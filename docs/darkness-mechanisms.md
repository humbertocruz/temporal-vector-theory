# Distinct Mechanisms of Darkness in Temporal Vector Theory

## Status

This document records a conceptual separation required by Temporal Vector Theory (TVT): dark matter and black holes may both be electromagnetically dark, but they are not dark for the same reason.

The distinction is provisional and must ultimately be expressed through a covariant dynamical model.

## 1. Two independent notions of darkness

TVT distinguishes at least two mechanisms:

1. **interaction darkness**, caused by weak overlap between local temporal-spatial frames;
2. **causal darkness**, caused by a horizon that prevents outward causal propagation.

They must not be identified.

## 2. Interaction darkness

Let visible matter have temporal orientation

```math
\mathbf u_{\mathrm{vis}}\approx(1,0,0),
```

and a dark excitation have orientation

```math
\mathbf u_{\mathrm{dark}}.
```

Define the relative alignment

```math
\chi_{\mathrm{vis,dark}}
=
\mathbf u_{\mathrm{vis}}\cdot\mathbf u_{\mathrm{dark}}.
```

A phenomenological non-gravitational coupling may depend on

```math
F_{\mathrm{int}}(\chi).
```

For weak alignment,

```math
F_{\mathrm{int}}(\chi)\approx0.
```

The excitation remains causally mobile within its own effective local section, but interacts only weakly with visible photons and visible matter.

This is the candidate TVT mechanism for dark matter.

## 3. Causal darkness

A black-hole horizon is not defined by weak electromagnetic overlap.

It is a hypersurface beyond which every future-directed causal trajectory remains inside the trapped region.

Introduce an outward causal-connectivity factor

```math
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{out}}[G,\mathbf u_T,\mathbf E_i].
```

Outside a horizon,

```math
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{out}}>0.
```

At the horizon,

```math
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{out}}=0.
```

Inside the horizon, there is no future-directed outward causal channel.

This is causal imprisonment, not sector misalignment.

## 4. Operational classification

Define an electromagnetic visibility factor

```math
\mathcal V
=
F_{\mathrm{int}}(\chi),
```

and an outward causal-connectivity factor

```math
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{out}}.
```

Then the minimal classification is:

### Visible matter

```math
\mathcal V>0,
\qquad
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{out}}>0.
```

### Dark matter

```math
\mathcal V\approx0,
\qquad
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{out}}>0.
```

### Black-hole interior

```math
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{out}}=0.
```

Its electromagnetic visibility is secondary because no causal signal can escape.

## 5. Geometric interpretation

Dark matter is associated with a stable local frame that has weak overlap with the visible frame:

```math
\mathcal S^{3,1}(\mathbf u_{\mathrm{dark}})
\not\approx
\mathcal S^{3,1}(\mathbf u_{\mathrm{vis}}).
```

A black hole is associated with extreme curvature or transport of local frames such that all future causal directions become trapped.

Therefore:

```math
\boxed{
\text{dark matter}
=
\text{weak interaction overlap}
}
```

while

```math
\boxed{
\text{black hole}
=
\text{loss of outward causal connectivity}
}
```

## 6. Reversible tilt versus sector transition

Velocity and gravity may tilt a particle's temporal-spatial frame without changing its internal sector identity.

A reversible kinematic or gravitational deformation may be represented by

```math
\mathbf u_T
\rightarrow
\mathbf u_T+\delta\mathbf u_T,
```

with the excitation returning to its original orientation after leaving the region.

This must not automatically be interpreted as conversion into dark matter.

A genuine sector transition would require a change between stable orientation states, such as

```math
\mathbf n_{\mathrm{matter}}:
(1,0,0)
\rightarrow
(0,1,0),
```

where `n_matter` is an internal sector-orientation variable distinct from ordinary kinematic tilt.

A complete theory must therefore distinguish:

- kinematic temporal orientation;
- gravitational transport of the local frame;
- internal sector orientation or temporal charge.

## 7. Black holes and dark matter can still interact

A black hole may accrete visible matter, dark matter, radiation, or other energy sources.

Once matter crosses the horizon, its original interaction sector is not directly recoverable from external observations. Externally, the black hole is characterized by its macroscopic gravitational parameters.

This does not imply

```math
\text{black hole}=\text{dark matter}.
```

It only means that both contribute gravitationally and may be electromagnetically dark for different reasons.

## 8. Primordial black holes

A population of primordial black holes could, in principle, behave as one component of cosmological dark matter.

In TVT this would be classified as a population of causally dark compact objects whose collective gravitational behavior mimics a dark-matter component.

This is distinct from the orientation-based dark sector proposed by TVT.

Therefore the theory permits two logically separate possibilities:

```math
\rho_{\mathrm{DM}}
=
\rho_{\mathrm{orientation\ sector}}
+
\rho_{\mathrm{compact\ objects}}
+
\cdots
```

without identifying their microscopic origins.

## 9. Research consequences

The separation suggests two independent mathematical tasks:

1. derive the interaction-overlap law `F_int(chi)`;
2. derive the horizon condition `C_out = 0` from the full metric and causal cone structure.

A viable TVT model must reproduce ordinary dark-matter mobility while also recovering the standard one-way causal structure of black-hole horizons.

## Provisional darkness-separation principle

> Electromagnetic darkness does not define a unique physical mechanism. In TVT, dark matter is provisionally associated with weak overlap between stable local temporal-spatial frames, whereas a black hole is defined by the loss of outward causal connectivity. Similar observational darkness must not be mistaken for identical ontology.
