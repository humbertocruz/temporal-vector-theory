# Open Problems

Temporal Vector Theory is incomplete. The following problems must be solved before it can be treated as a physical theory rather than a geometric interpretation.

## 1. Covariant formulation

The current parametrization uses relative speed `v`. A complete formulation must define temporal orientation without introducing a preferred frame.

## 2. Metric signature

The geometry of the three temporal components is not yet fixed. Candidate structures include Euclidean internal geometry, hyperbolic geometry, pseudo-Riemannian signatures, or a fiber-space construction over ordinary spacetime.

## 3. Dynamics

The theory lacks an action principle, field equations, conserved currents, and equations of motion for `\mathbf u_T`.

## 4. Stability

Extra timelike degrees of freedom often produce ghosts, negative-energy states, causal pathologies, or unstable initial-value problems. TVT must explicitly avoid these failures.

## 5. Matter coupling

No derived law currently connects temporal orientation to electric charge, spin, particle family, antimatter, dark matter, or dark energy.

## 6. General relativity

A viable theory must explain how temporal orientation couples to spacetime curvature and whether the equivalence principle survives.

## 7. Quantum consistency

The framework must eventually address unitarity, locality, causality, quantization, and compatibility with quantum field theory.

## 8. Distinct prediction

The current equations reproduce known relativistic identities. At least one measurable deviation from established theory is required.

## 9. Operational definitions

Every new quantity must be tied to a possible measurement procedure. Hidden coordinates that can never affect observables are mathematical redundancy, not additional physics.

## 10. Terminology

Terms such as hidden time, anti-time, dark time, and anti-space remain provisional and must not be confused with established physical entities.
